Austrian Glühwein

German:    glühen = to glow,   der Wein = wine

 

a.         1 bottle wine, red, full-bodied, cheap (See Note 1)

b.         1 cinnamon stick, approx 3 inches (~8 cm) long if  thick (~2.5 cm diameter)

                        or approx 5 inches (~12 cm) long if thin  (~1.5 cm diameter).   See the “Tip”, below.

c.         8-12 cloves (whole)

d.                  1 small juicy orange, sliced

e.                   (Optional). One lump (i.e. approx. 3cm x 3cm x 3cm) stem ginger (from syrup), chopped.

f.                    Approx 2+ Tablespoons sugar, slightly/moderately heaped (Note 2)

g.                  A dash of Cognac (optional)

 

Tip      Be careful with the cinnamon stick/s. Cinnamon is a powerful flavour, delicious if you get it right, overpowering if you use too much.

Thin cinnamon sticks are available in small jars from the Spices section of most supermarkets, e.g. Sainsbury’s.

Thicker individual sticks are available from most delicatessens.

 

Put ingredients a-d, or a-e, into a stainless-steel saucepan with a lid, and heat until it just starts to boil all over (not just at the edges). Immediately remove from the heat, and allow to stand for five minutes, with the pan lid still on, so that the wine can soak up the flavours from the spices and orange without losing the alcohol by evaporation. Then add 2 tablespoons of  sugar, slightly/moderately heaped, and stir to dissolve. With the pan lid on, re-heat just to boiling point. Test the flavour, and stir in a small amount of extra sugar (usually between zero and half-a-tablespoon) to the extent that is needed, aiming for a flavour that is spicey, and sweet-and-sour, all at the same time (see Note 2 again).

 

Pass the Glühwein through a sieve into a Pyrex jug, to remove the cloves etc, then serve out into wine glasses. Use ordinary cheap pub wine glasses, not expensive cut-glass wine glasses which might crack with the heat.

 

Allow your guests to decide how much -- if any at all -- brandy they want to add to their individual glasses. I allow them to help themselves, as they think fit. But recommend to them about one thimbleful per glass, advising them that a small quantity adds to the flavour, but that too much will overpower the flavour of the Glühwein.

 

Note 1. Cheap, because you should not pay good money for a bouquet or a finish that will become undetectable once you have added the cinnamon stick and cloves. However, avoid the very light red wines such as new-season Beaujolais or Piat d'Or. I find that a cheaper version of supermarket Corbière wine is excellent, being both cheap and full-bodied. Many of the cheaper versions of supermarket Chilean and Argentinian wine are equally satisfactory.

Note 2. This is left to your individual judgement. But remember that too much sugar can rob the Glühwein of character, while too little sugar makes it unpleasantly astringent.
