Where does the game come from?

 

I know very little about where the game of Hey-up game has come from.

 

A Mr Dombrowski – a friend at the time, but after 50 years I have forgotten his Christian name – and his wife, taught me how to play Hey-up when we were (all three of us, plus a few others) on holiday together, camping in Iceland for ten days during the summer of 1971. I think I remember, but cannot be sure, that my friends mentioned that they had learnt the game from a Moroccan in Casablanca the year before. I am even more uncertain about the following, but I think I also remember them mentioning that the Moroccan had told them that he (the Moroccan) had learnt the game from an Austrian he met in Innsbruck. To be honest, after 50 years I am not 100% sure that his surname was actually Dombrowski, although it was certainly a Polish surname.

 

Despite his Polish surname, I thought of Mr Dombrowski as being as British as myself. For all I know, he might be the fourth or fifth generation down from the original Dombrowski who emigrated from Poland to Britain.  But he was about the same age as myself, which means that he must have been born in 1942, give or take a few years. He could therefore be the son of one of the many Poles who arrived in Britain at the start of the war in 1939. If this is the case, the game could be Polish, handed down to him by his parents.

 

The outcome of all this is that the game of Hey-up could be (in increasing order of likelihood) Icelandic, British,  Moroccan, Austrian or Polish. I have been unable to discover which.

 

On my return to England, I did not think about Hey-up again until a few years later. It must have been around 1974 when I thought it might be a good idea to play the game again, to entertain  some friends of that time. Unable to remember the fine detail of the original rules, I improvised the rules about the specific details of the exceptional cards which, I decided, would from that time onwards be Jack, 8 and 2. These replacement rules, in principle but not necessarily in their fine detail, corresponded as accurately as I could remember with the rules that we used in Iceland, which similarly specified three exceptional cards. I have played the game with my own substituted rules, sporadically, ever since 1974. In 2018, my son-in-law asked me for a printed copy of the rules, and the “description and rules” webpage listed at the top of this page was the result. To prepare it, I  googled the Internet to try to find the original rules, but could find no mention of the game -- however I spelt it, from hey-up (English) to jé-op (Spanish) and beyond.  However, I do not know how to spell “hey-up” in German – can any reader help on this for a renewed Google search, in case the game really is Austrian? And how the French and Arabic speakers of Morocco might spell “hey-up”  is also a mystery, because French does not have much of an “h” sound. For the French possibility,  I have tried googling “allez-hop”, but the results, though interesting,    --    alley oop - Wiktionary, the free dictionary --  did not include the card game. The expression does, however, indicate that an action (but not the playing of a card) is required exactly on the “p” of “hop”, which is encouraging. There is an intriguing similarity here with the game of Hey-up, which also requires an action, the playing of a card,  exactly on the “p” of “up”.

 

I believe that I, and my family and close friends, might possibly be the sole remaining guardians, worldwide, of this entertaining game. Hey-up  seems to be in danger of becoming extinct. I can find no evidence on the internet that anybody in the world is still playing it, which I find very sad. I have decided  to undertake the project of trying to prevent the extinction of this fine game. To do this, I am trying to make the game “go viral” on the internet. If you have enjoyed the game, please help me by “sharing” or “liking” on Facebook.

 

This brilliant game is in danger of becoming extinct.  If you have played the game and enjoyed it, please keep it alive by visiting  www.facebook.com/richardchamberscardgames, where you can help by clicking “like”, or (even better) by sharing this page with your Facebook friends.
