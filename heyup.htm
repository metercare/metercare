The rules of the card game of Hey-up

Keywords  hey-up, “hey up”, hey up,  cards, card game, Leeds, York, Dick, Richard, Chambers, “Dick Chambers”, Dick Chambers, noisy, fun, boisterous, sociable

A quick-paced, sociable and rather noisy card game for a minimum of 5 and a maximum of 12 players.  The game works best for 6 – 10 players.

 

Is this  game suitable for you?

            Not suitable for any person who dislikes noise.

Not suitable if the noise is likely to annoy your neighbours, e.g. if you live in a flat with thin walls.

Not recommended for any person who easily becomes flustered, and as a result becomes upset. This game is 100% fluster.

You need “workable” eyesight (but not necessarily A1 perfect) so you can quickly see the cards played by the players up to two positions to your left or right.

           

Suitable for everybody else, including children aged 10+ years (estimated age, depending upon the child).

The game is simply for fun, and does NOT include any gambling.

 

 

Game description and rules

Hey-up is a sociable and noisy card game, for which you need a constant alertness in a situation that changes frequently and with unpredictable suddenness. You also need an ability to make an accurate decision about what to do in response to each new situation as it arises, and to act upon it quickly and calmly within a severely limited time-scale. Nevertheless, the game is simply for fun, and should not be taken too seriously. Although winning is always pleasant, the main feature of Hey-up is its sociability and the civilised release of inhibitions when chanting “Hey-up”. The chanting of hey-up, in unison with everybody else, can be therapeutic. The game will occupy you so fully that you will have no other worries while you are playing it.

 

Deal 8 cards to each player – so you need to use two packs of cards. Any card that has not been dealt is held in a central pile. The object is to be the first to get rid of all your cards.

 

The pace of the game is set by the steady rhythmic chant of  a loud "Heeeeey-up, Heeeeey-up, . . . ", voiced collectively, and without inhibition,  by all the players at the rate of about 25 to 30 per minute. Each “Hey” is stretched into “Heeeeey”  until you get this approximate rate.  (You might like to practise this against a stop watch, to work out the recommended pace of the game). The interval between one “hey up” and the next is your thinking time. If you believe it is your turn to play a card, you must play it, “with cool”, exactly on the "p" of "up".

 

First group of rules. If you:-

play your card non-rhythmically, i.e. noticeably before or after the "p" of "up";

play your card in a “non-cool” way, e.g. in a panicky or snatchy fashion;

fail to play a card when it is your turn;

play a card when it is not your turn;

play an incorrect card;

or, having played your card, fail to remove your hand with the result that it obscures the view of the card for the neighbouring players;

and if you are challenged for it (the challenge must be made before the “p” of the next “hey-up”) by another player, then you must pick up one card from the central pile, plus accept one card from your first challenger. But any player who interrupts the game by making an incorrect or unjustified challenge will have to pick up one card from the central pile.

 

Second group of rules. Which card to play? The card you play must either:-

be the same colour (red or black) as the card played by the previous player

or be the same value as the card played by the previous player, irrespective of its colour;

or one greater;

or one less;

For example, if the previous player played 4 Diamonds, you could play any Diamond or any Heart (both red), or you could play a 3, 4, or 5 of any suit, black or red. You could not play, for example, the 10 Spades (which is the wrong colour, and is not within 1 of the previous card)

.

The Ace is high or low. i.e. one greater than a King, and one less than a 2.

 

Third group of rules. There are three important exceptional cards (Jack, 8 and 2):-

a.       Any Jack. – reverses the direction of play

Aide memoire:  A jack-knife is a knife (such as a penknife).  The blade of a jack knife may point along the direction of the handle at one moment, then a moment later it might be folded back into the handle --  the reverse direction.

After a Jack is played, the direction of play immediately reverses, or jack-knives.  e.g. if the play had previously been progressing clockwise (from right to left), it reverses to anti-clockwise (left to right). It will remain anticlockwise until either another Jack is played, or until a session is ended by a player making a challenge and somebody having to pick up a card from the central pile. The new session always starts off clockwise (from right to left).

b.       Any 8. – produces a hiatus in the play.

Aide-memoire:    If she plays an eight,

                            You’ll have to wait               (William Wordsworth, Elegy to a Game of Cards, 1803)

The playing of an 8 produces a hiatus (hi-8-tus, perhaps?) in the playing of the cards, but not in the chanting of “hey-up”. After an 8 has been played, the next player in the proper order must delay his play by one "hey-up". That is, he/she does not play a card on the immediate next "up", but does so exactly on the "up" that comes after that.

c.       Any 2  -- causes the next player in the normal order of play  to miss his/her turn

Aide-memoire: The sudden appearance of a two  (“2”) implies that a one (“1”) might be missing. This is reflected in the play by making the next player (No 1) miss a turn, which is taken by the next-but-one player (No 2) instead.

Therefore:  after a 2 has been played, the next person in the normal order of play (No 1) must miss his turn, and the player after him (No 2) must play his card instead, “with cool”, exactly on the “p” of “ hey-up”.

 

e.g. suppose that

Mark              Janet                      Mary

- - - - - - - - - - - -> - - - - - - - - - - - ->

are sitting at the table in that order, and that the play at that time happens to be anticlockwise (i.e. from left to right). Suppose Mark plays a 2. Then Janet must not play a card, but Mary plays her card instead, playing “with cool”, exactly on the "p" of the next "Hey-up".
